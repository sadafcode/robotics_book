# Humanoid Locomotion: Walking and Running

Creating stable, dynamic walking is the holy grail of humanoid robotics. This chapter covers the theoretical foundations and practical implementations of bipedal locomotion.

---

## 1. The Challenge of Bipedal Walking

<ContentSection levels={['non_technical', 'beginner']}>

Walking is actually a controlled fall. When you walk, you lean forward and catch yourself with each step. A robot must:
- Continuously calculate balance
- Coordinate dozens of joints
- Handle unexpected bumps and slopes
- Recover from slips

This is much harder than wheeled robots, which are naturally stable.

</ContentSection>

<ContentSection levels={['intermediate', 'professional']}>

Bipedal locomotion is fundamentally unstable — unlike wheeled robots, humanoids are **underactuated** during parts of the gait cycle. The system is a controlled inverted pendulum that must maintain dynamic stability.

</ContentSection>

---

## 2. Gait Phases

<ContentSection levels={['non_technical', 'beginner']}>

Walking has two main phases:
- **Double Support**: Both feet on the ground (stable, like standing)
- **Single Support**: One foot on ground, other swinging forward

The robot constantly switches between these, making balance calculations critical.

</ContentSection>

<ContentSection levels={['intermediate', 'professional']}>

### Walking Gait Cycle

```mermaid
graph LR
    A[Double Support] -->|Shift Weight| B[Single Support - Right]
    B -->|Swing Left Leg| C[Double Support]
    C -->|Shift Weight| D[Single Support - Left]
    D -->|Swing Right Leg| A
```

**Double Support**: Both feet in ground contact. CoM is well-controlled.

**Single Support**: One foot supports entire weight. The swinging foot moves forward while the support foot maintains balance.

</ContentSection>

## 3. Zero Moment Point (ZMP)

<ContentSection levels={['non_technical', 'beginner']}>

**ZMP** is a fancy way to check if the robot is about to fall.

Imagine the robot's weight pushing down through its feet. If that push is centered, the robot is stable. If it moves to the edge of the foot, the robot starts to tip over.

**Rule**: Keep the ZMP inside the foot's support area.

</ContentSection>

<ContentSection levels={['intermediate', 'professional']}>

The **Zero Moment Point** is where the horizontal moment of ground reaction forces equals zero.

$$\tau_x = 0, \quad \tau_y = 0$$

For dynamic stability during walking, the ZMP must remain within the **support polygon** (convex hull of contact points).

### Linear Inverted Pendulum Model (LIPM)

The simplified dynamics model for walking:

$$\ddot{x} = \frac{g}{z_c}(x - x_{zmp})$$

Where:
- $x$: CoM horizontal position
- $z_c$: Constant CoM height
- $g$: Gravity
- $x_{zmp}$: ZMP position

This lets us plan CoM trajectories by specifying desired ZMP positions.

</ContentSection>

## 5. Modern Approaches

<ContentSection levels={['non_technical', 'beginner']}>

Modern humanoid robots use advanced techniques:
- **Model Predictive Control (MPC)**: Plan several steps ahead, like a chess player
- **Reinforcement Learning**: Let the robot learn to walk through trial and error
- **Hybrid approaches**: Combine physics models with learned behaviors

</ContentSection>

<ContentSection levels={['intermediate', 'professional']}>

### Model Predictive Control (MPC)

Optimizes control inputs over a receding horizon:

$$\min_{u} \sum_{k=0}^{N} \|x_k - x_{ref}\|_Q^2 + \|u_k\|_R^2$$

Subject to dynamics constraints and contact feasibility.

### Reinforcement Learning

Recent breakthroughs (2024-2025) use RL to learn robust walking policies:
- **Sim-to-real transfer**: Train in Isaac Sim, deploy on real robot
- **Domain randomization**: Vary friction, mass, terrain during training
- **Adversarial training**: Add perturbations to learn recovery behaviors

:::tip
Unitree H1 and Boston Dynamics Atlas use hybrid approaches — model-based walking controllers with learned balance recovery.
:::

</ContentSection>

## Assessment

<ContentSection levels={['beginner', 'intermediate', 'professional']}>

**Q1**: Why is walking a "controlled fall"?
- *Answer: The CoM is outside the support polygon during parts of the gait — the robot is constantly catching itself*

**Q2**: What happens if ZMP leaves the support polygon?
- *Answer: The robot generates a tipping moment and will fall unless a recovery step is taken*

**Q3**: What is the advantage of MPC over fixed gait patterns?
- *Answer: MPC can adapt to disturbances and terrain changes in real-time by replanning*
